Day 3: 20th December.

This day was the last day of the whole conference. Whole new idealogies and techniques in the robotics world were discussed and many were to be discussed yet more. It was a grand finale of what all happened the following days, So It was started of grandly itself with the plenary talk by Jeffrey Sachs.

Dr. Jeffrey Sachs is a world renowned economist from the Earth Institute, and gave his talk via a live Skype call to the audience. He congratulated Amrita University on the work that is being done by them. He began his talk by saying that India is one of the pioneers in the field of IT, and is now going through a demonetization phase. He believes that India will have a historic transformation in the coming year, and recommended that debit cards be replaced with thumb prints. He also greatly described the different Sustainable Development Goals and how India can achieve all of them. He said that by developing cutting edge technologies, India can surely achieve the SDGs. The government of India has said that all of the village in the country will get electricity by May 2017. That is about 6000 villages, and he said a challenge for India is to provide energy that is clean. He said all the SDGs eventually tie back to SDG 1, which is a No Poverty. To end his talk, he said that india can do a lot with Africa to achieve SDGs, and he encouraged this collaboration to happen. Then it was by Professor Subir Kumar Saha from IIT Delhi started his keynote by introducing IIT Delhi and his lab. He showed video of some of the robotics equipment in his lab. He explained the concept of robotics for education and his collaboration with Japan, and also gave an idea about mechatronics, simulators and its applications. He showcased his publications and research activities happening in his lab that includes the involvement of his team in robotics competitions and paper presentations. He also showed a demonstration of a carpet scrapping machine (Carpet weaving is facing a tough competition from other exporting countries of Asia-Pacific region due to market liberalisation. A project with the aims to improve processes, tools and machines involved in the manufacturing of carpets was initiated by IIT Delhi in 2000. In the project, optimisation of a metallic loom was carried out for its reduced weight and cost.), which provided a systematic approach to improve the existing tools and processes used by artisans engaged in carpet sectors. This effort had been initiated in 2000 by IIT Delhi, and today more than 250 looms are in use in several places across

India that are developed by them.

Then it was the turn of Brent Gillespie from University of Michigan had his research in the area of the haptic interface, where he applied techniques from multibody dynamics, nonlinear controls, and robotics. He introduced the virtual teacher, a device or agent that supplements an environment in order to facilitate acquisition by a human user of a manual skill. A virtual teacher generally acts as an aide or facilitator to task execution and is present only during training periods. During eventual task performance the teacher is absent. The virtual teacher’s objective is to promote independent mastery over the task. He showed the different application of this teacher and pupil relationship in robotics, which was very interesting. He proposed a model-based cancellation controller to reduce the effects of biodynamic feedthrough where the controller processes vehicle acceleration measurements through a transfer function model of the human biodynamics, and a motor imposes the result as the torque that acts directly on the manual control interface. This had a positive effect on the disturbance response of the driver-vehicle system.


Dr Sunil Agrawal, Professor, Columbia University, was next and presented broad highlights of the research going on in Robotics and Rehabilitation Laboratory (ROAR) and Robotics System Engineering Laboratory (RSEL) of Columbia University. The main objectives of ROAR lab are to design novel exoskeletons and robotic braces and focus on how neuromuscular systems respond in new human environments. He showed snapshots of different products developed in the lab such as dynamic spine brace for correcting upper body postures which can be used to treat patients suffering from cerebral palsy and scoliosis, and dynamic Neck brace for treating patients suffering from Parkinson's and Cervical Dystonia. These exoskeletons were also used as an assistive device for gait training of stroke patients with paralysis to correct their asymmetric movements. Clinical outcomes were shown and he stated that there are significant differences before and after training. Another important product he introduced was the wireless shoes used for gait analysis and feedback.

The Keynote speaker Dr Ashish Dutta, Professor, IIT Kanpur has presented the design and control of two fingers and three finger exoskeleton for stroke and rehabilitation. These designs are based on finger motion trajectory in order to emulate the motion of varying instantaneous axis. The main motivation behind these exoskeletons is to develop the connection between brain and kinematics of the exoskeleton during the physiotherapy. Dr Ashish Dutta had shown the importance of BCI paradigms to establish the control of fingers exoskeleton and the patient. He stated that the BCI interface forces the patient to focus on physiotherapy exercises and the results showed that the patient’s recovery is improved by adopting this method. The functions of physiotherapist were replaced by the exoskeletons. He showed different versions of the exoskeletons used; three finger exoskeleton was used for translation and rotation movements and a simplified two finger exoskeleton for open and close movements of the hand.


Ashokan Thondiyath, He started the talk by pointing the issues in underwater facilities like deep water horizontal oil spills, loss of internet connection due to cyclone and difficulty in detection of missing ships and flights and the need for deployment of sensors. Even though there are many underwater robots to solve this issues, they are complex and expensive. So their aim was to develop a simpler underwater robot for the humanitarian purpose. He discussed the existing autonomous underwater robots ROVs and AUVs and the limitations with them. He told that they have already implemented and tested several remotely controlled and operated robots, even one for defence, but it was not really helpful. This inspired them to develop much simpler method rather than robots. “We don’t really need robots to solve the underwater facilities; we can use much simpler technologies instead,” Dr Ashokan said. They aimed to develop a suitable deployment system for underwater observation system for selective deployment operation. Thus they have introduced a variable buoyancy (VB) system for selective under water deployments like surveillance, search and rescue operations.

The concept of the proposed system was to change the buoyancy of an object according to the depth and to control its up and down motion. He also explained the various applications of the proposed system, such as standalone VB modules with hooks for salvaging, cable connected VB modules for selective positioning of sensors, modules for back packs for divers etc. The proposed system was a combination different modules with specific tasks which are connected to the base station. And also, to study the system they have done mathematical modelling and tested it. He said that if he has a ship with multiple modules, he can add it on different depths and can collect the info at a different position. He concluded that buoyancy forces in underwater can be effectively used for underwater wave motion and variable buoyancy modules can be developed as a stand-alone system for a selective mission.

Dr Shankar our Dean, talked about the various projects that are at Amrita University, and the impact that they have on the people who they are meant to help. He mentioned that Amrita University is just 14 years old, and has a ranking as the number 1 private university in India. His talk followed a discussion of what makes Amrita University grow so fast. It is because Amrita has a specific mission and target goal that differentiates them from other universities. He discussed the haptic technologies and women empowerment projects of AMMACHI Labs and their impact on the rural populations. He pointed out a statistic that showed that if we train the men, the output will be 40%, but for women, it is 100%. He also talked about the Amrita Wireless Networks (WNA) in disaster management and landslide detection, as well as Amrita’s Humanitarian Technologies Lab (HuT). He concluded with a beautiful quote by Amma, the Chancellor of Amrita University, and discussed the science behind humanitarianism. It was a poignant way to end the last keynote address of RAHA 2016.
