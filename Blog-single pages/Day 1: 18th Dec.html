Day 1: 18th Dec.

Life, Fun



Day 1: 18th December 2016.

In the following coming blogs, I will be writing about what happened during the conference according to the schedule and will write briefly about what the speakers and the delegate spoke about the robotics and their projects during the conference.

As I said I will brief you guys about what these speakers talked during these 3 days in 3 different blog posts. The day started of by a warm welcome to all the speakers, delegates and other. We all had the breakfast and entered the sessions. First was the plenary talk by Ronald Arkin.

He gave a very detailed overview on the applications of robotics in different areas like military, health care etc. and the challenges in developing (architecture) such robots not by compromising the safety and security of the human beings. He started with a phrase “100 dollar laptops failed when cell phones have gained popularity. Why?” The first part of his talk dealt with talking about the military use of robots, especially in terms of reducing innocent casualties. He mentioned that a big challenge was to ensure that the robots obey humanitarian laws to ensure minimal loss of life to these innocent people. The second part of his talk was around ways that the way that robots could deal with diseases such as Parkinson’s disease and stigmatization. He also showed demos of various robots that could detect emotion, such as interjecting when someone raises their voice in anger, and also to stop playing when detecting anger or other negative emotions. The final part of his talk was about deception, and how deception-based models in robots can be good.


After lunch, Next was the plenary talk by bradley nelson followed by Bernardine Dias.


Dr. Brad Nelson was presented via skype from Hong Kong. He gave a online talk via skype from Hong Kong. He started his talk by acknowledging his team members from Multiscale robotics labs (MSRL). He gave a small glimpse of the works happening in their lab where they try to understand the mechanobiology of very small devices, magnetics based medical robots, micro and nano robots, material and fabrications. He went ahead with Micro-nano robotics for medical diagnostics and analysis. He took us through the basic terms and its history like Cyborg surgeons which was projected as half machine and half surgeons through advertisements. Prof. Russ Taylor from John Hopkins has been regarded as the father of medical robotics which works on 2 loops the inner loop which is mostly used and outer loop which deals with feedbacks and regarded as a time taking process. In his talk he focussed on the inner loop component which is now used in different areas in the field of medicine. It was in the 2000s that the first commercial device with inner loop was come into market and this might have got inspiration from the old english movie Fantastic voyage(1966),

in which the machines are shrunk to understand what is inside the blood vessels. These devices use the concept of magnetism where forces (changes in magnetism) and torque (rotating) play a major role. He looked at eye with cataract and fixed vision with electromagnets.which in the 1974 was done by injecting into the lens and spin to liquefy the lens nucleus. First prototype that his team had developed was octomag which could very precisely control the small device with 8 diff coils. An example he used was on a disease condition Age-related macular degeneration(AMD)which occurs the retinal cells of elderly people forming vasculature. Treatment was to inject medicines like Avastin, Lucentis etc. into the ocular cavity but was found to be not specific to vasculature area. That is when microbots started taking the place of medicines which are specific and tested on animal models like rabbit eye that were magnetically driven. He continued his talk with another example i.e. heart where catheters are used to make ablations based on forces and torques put forward on kidney by Hans Tillande in 1951--catheter in kidney systems. Aeon phocus, an electromagnetic catheter steering systems which was first installed in Vitecan. “SDG(sustainable Development Goal) cannot be achieved without the explicitly addressing one of the most crucial needs facing the world: a lack of access to surgery”. He then went forward explaining the application of using both octomag and catheter to capsulorhexis to treat cataract achieved by injecting the device for that a prototype device with 4+3 DOF was designed. Flagellum based bots can also be build where it can swim in different direction and also generate the force and torque based on the motion. But all these things have different challenges like viewing surgery in the eye, safety measures. He also includes as a general information for the eye there is no vision based bots as the smallest camera is 1mm cube.

Dr. Bernardine Dias was an associate research professor at the Robotics Institute at Carnegie Mellon University and the creator and director of TechBridgeWorld. She delivered a talk on ‘Prioritizing Humans in Humanitarian Technology Applications’. Her work is basically on innovative technology that empowers underserved communities in a manner that is culturally relevant and locally sustainable. Her contributions in this field have aided to understand how technology bridge the world. She gave a brief idea about TechBridgeWorld, a program at Carnegie Mellon University in which students use technology to help underdeveloped countries. Also she mentioned about iSTEP (Innovative Student Technology ExPerience), a student internship program and their innovations that they put to use in countries like Tanzania and Ghana. Her team have developed the Electronic Braille Tutor, a device that help the blind population in learning written braille. “In Tanzania, many teachers of blind students are blind themselves, and if the audio is there to correct any mistakes, it saves the teacher from having to rewrite the students’ work themselves,” she added.

This inspired them to add the audio feature to the Braille Tutor and it became very useful in training blind students. In their study, they have used six different types of Braille. Students were asked to read and write using this and they have evaluated their performance by conducting exams. They have noticed that, the performance extended far beyond making proper sentence syntax. This device can easily map to different languages and is been tested all over the world, she said. She stated that “technology alone will solve nothing; rather it need socio-economic contents to make a good relationship”.

After that it was Meg Johns and Paul Verschure turn to delight us with thier awesome talk and they did.

Meg Jones, Chief of UN Women, started her talk by thanking RAHA coordinators and mentioned RAHA as the start of the big journey towards Robotics for Humanitarian applications. She explained the transition from Millennium Development Goals (MEG) to Sustainable development Goals (SEG) and presented the 17 key areas identified by UN to achieve the SDG September 2015. She pointed out that UN is looking to see how India partner with other countries and explained UN Women strategies to enhance the participation of women in key areas and to establish companies owned by women in India. She exemplified AMMACHI labs project on coconut climbing as part of UN chapter to promote sustainable agriculture. Women all over the world are suffering due to different social and economic factors such as lack of infrastructure, discriminatory social norms, male preferences, early marriage, related health issues and so on. She explained the UN solutions to the above problems by leveraging ICTS to ensure access to quality education and training for women and girls everywhere and pointed out the identified targets as adolescent girls, indigenous people

and refugees. Investing on women and girls is worthwhile since they reinvest 90% into their families and educated women build the foundations of an educated society by educating their children. She concluded by telling that UN Women focus on Gender Equality not on Women equality.

Paul Verschure classified ‘Living Machines’ as the paradigm of developing technology to build a dignified sustainable society. The talk continued from network science to application oriented robotics that is useful to the society, with underlying principles and considerations. By indicating examples such as rehabilitation virtual reality games at Barcelona hospital and historical learning initiatives using tablet education in Germany, he pointed out that interaction with robots and virtual reality training systems can improve the life of humans. Robots and virtual reality in classrooms and how these help in the education is one of the theme line of his lab. He talked on the use of reconstruction as a portal to access to historical sources or information which is then given to students to explore for education. Showed videos on children interacting with obots which explains how body functions and how the body responds for the exercise . He explained the need of bioinspired approaches to build such machines to fill the gap between brain and mind. Inspired from the most complex system of human body; the Brain; and its multiscale principles, his lab builds robots that works on

Distributed Adaptive Control (DAC) models trying to use robots to understand the link between machines and mind. Research on error based learning in the brain by using classical conditioning and its application to neuro prosthetics was introduced. He also differentiated different methods of therapy, based on Rehabilitation Gaming System (RGS) and Constrained Induced Movement Therapy (CIMT).

After this it was paper presentation followed by cultural program an Amazing Bharathanatyam performance by Deepthi Parol and daughter Avyaya Parol. And the day was ended with a beautiful Banquet dinner.
